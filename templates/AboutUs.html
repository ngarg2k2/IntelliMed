We are developing a system that has the potential to revolutionize healthcare delivery. Our system accurately predicts diseases based on symptoms and recommends appropriate medicines using machine learning techniques such as Random Forest models. These models analyze large amounts of medical data and identify patterns and relationships between symptoms, diseases, and medicines used to treat them.


Our system is designed to help people purchase medicine from a smart store. The store's inventory of medicines is updated based on predictions of previous usage, ensuring that the store always has enough stock of the most frequently used medicines while avoiding unnecessary stock of less frequently used ones. This approach optimizes medicine inventory management, reduces costs and waste associated with medicine inventory management, and improves the availability of medicines for patients.


Machine learning techniques like LSTM models have the potential to improve healthcare outcomes and patient satisfaction, particularly in developing countries where access to medical professionals and resources is limited. Our system can provide a reliable and efficient solution for disease prediction and medicine recommendation, potentially improving healthcare outcomes and making healthcare more accessible to everyone.


Our system can reduce the burden on medical professionals by providing accurate and reliable disease predictions and medicine recommendations. This could potentially reduce the strain on the healthcare system, allowing medical professionals to focus on more complex cases.


In conclusion, our system has the potential to improve healthcare outcomes, reduce costs and waste associated with medicine inventory management, and make healthcare more accessible to everyone. By leveraging the power of advanced machine learning techniques such as LSTM models, we can provide a reliable and efficient system for disease prediction and medicine recommendation that can revolutionize the way healthcare is delivered around the world.
